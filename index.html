Unit 1 Scientific Method, Bias & Scale
Science vs Pseudoscience & Types of Bias
▶

Science is a process, not just a collection of facts. A scientific claim must be testable, falsifiable, and reproducible. If someone else follows the same method and gets the same result, the claim is supported. If the claim cannot in principle be tested or shown to be wrong, it is not scientific.

Pseudoscience looks like science on the surface but fails these tests. Common signs: relies on personal stories (anecdotes) rather than controlled data, cannot be proven wrong no matter what, uses scientific-sounding language without real evidence, or the evidence is cherry-picked to support a conclusion already held.

Types of bias
  • Confirmation bias: The researcher only notices or records evidence that supports their existing belief, and ignores anything that contradicts it.
  • Sampling bias: The group of people or objects studied does not fairly represent the full population you want to draw conclusions about.
  • Reporting bias: Studies with positive or dramatic results get published; studies that found nothing do not, giving a distorted picture of the evidence.
  • Observer bias: The person measuring a result is influenced by what they expect to see, often unconsciously.
Key test for pseudoscience: Ask "what evidence would prove this wrong?" If there is no possible answer, it is not science.

Sampling — Why We Sample & How to Do It Well
▶

Why we sample: It is almost never possible to study every individual in a population. Sampling lets us study a manageable group and use the results to make reasonable inferences about the whole population — as long as the sample is chosen well.

A good sample must be large enough to reduce the effect of random variation, and it must be representative — the people or things chosen should reflect the full diversity of the population. A random sample (where every individual has an equal chance of being chosen) is the gold standard.

Common sampling mistakes
  • Sample too small: A few data points can be misleading due to chance.
  • Not random: Asking only your friends, or only people who volunteer, introduces bias — certain groups will be over or under represented.
  • Wrong population: Studying teenagers to make claims about all adults is not valid.
Remember: Even a large sample can be biased if it is not representative. 10,000 responses from one country cannot represent the global population.

Scale Factor
▶

Scale factor describes the relationship between the size of a model and the size of the real object. It is written as a ratio, for example 1:100, which means 1 unit on the model equals 100 units in reality. The model is 100 times smaller than the real object.

Scale factor = model size / real size Real size = model size / scale factor Model size = real size x scale factor
Direction matters

A scale of 1:100 means the model is smaller. A scale of 100:1 means the model is larger than the real thing (used in microscopy — a microscope image might be 1000:1 to make tiny structures visible).

Common mistake: Students sometimes flip the ratio. In 1:100, the model is 100x smaller. The real object is 100x bigger, not smaller.

Unit 2 Atomic Structure & Fundamental Forces
Scale & Scientific Notation
▶

The universe operates across an enormous range of scales — from quarks (10-18 m) to the observable universe (1026 m). We use scientific notation to handle very large and very small numbers without writing endless zeros.

Scientific notation writes a number as a value between 1 and 10 multiplied by a power of 10. For example: 6,400,000 = 6.4 x 106, and 0.000035 = 3.5 x 10-5.

a x 10^n where 1 ≤ a < 10
Reading the exponent
  • Positive exponent (e.g. 106): large number, move decimal right.
  • Negative exponent (e.g. 10-9): tiny number, move decimal left.

Atoms & the Periodic Table
▶

An atom has a dense central nucleus containing protons (positive) and neutrons (no charge), surrounded by electrons (negative) arranged in shells. The number of protons defines which element it is.

Atomic number = number of protons Mass number = protons + neutrons Neutrons = mass number - atomic number
Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons. They have the same chemical behaviour (same electrons, same bonding) but different masses. The atomic mass on the periodic table is the weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes — which is why it is often a decimal, not a whole number.

Structure of the periodic table
  • Period (row): Tells you how many electron shells the atom has.
  • Group (column): Tells you how many valence (outer shell) electrons the atom has.
  • Electron configuration: Shells fill from inside out: shell 1 holds max 2, shell 2 holds max 8, shell 3 holds max 8.

Fundamental Particles & the Four Forces
▶

Protons and neutrons are not fundamental — they are made of smaller particles called quarks. A proton contains 2 up quarks and 1 down quark. A neutron contains 1 up quark and 2 down quarks. Quarks are held together by the strong nuclear force, carried by particles called gluons.

The four fundamental forces
ForceCarrier particleRangeRelative strength
GravitationalGravitonInfiniteWeakest of all four
ElectromagneticPhotonInfiniteStrong at atomic scale
Strong nuclearGluonVery short (inside nucleus)Strongest at very short range
Weak nuclearW/Z bosonsVery shortResponsible for radioactive decay
Common mistake: Quarks are held together by the strong nuclear force, not gravity. Gravity is far too weak at subatomic scales. Gravity only becomes important for very massive objects like planets and stars.

7-8Calculate
Q3

Calculate + Justify
▶
Answer

Weight & Gravitational Force
▶

Mass is the amount of matter in an object. It is measured in kilograms and never changes regardless of where the object is. Weight is the gravitational force acting on that mass. It changes depending on the strength of gravity (e.g. your weight on the Moon is about 1/6 of your weight on Earth, but your mass is exactly the same).

W = mg (Weight = mass x gravitational field strength) F = G m1 m2 / r^2 (Newton's Law of Gravitation)

In Newton's Law: G = 6.674 x 10-11 N m2 kg-2, m1 and m2 are the two masses in kg, r is the distance between their centres in metres.

Unit 3 Periodic Table, Ions & Taxonomy
Trends in the Periodic Table
▶
PropertyAcross a period (left to right)Down a group
Atomic radiusDecreases (more protons pull electrons closer)Increases (more shells)
ElectronegativityIncreasesDecreases

Electronegativity (X) measures how strongly an atom pulls on electrons in a bond. Scale 0 to 4. Fluorine has the highest (3.98), Francium the lowest (0.7). A large difference in X between two atoms means a strong, possibly explosive reaction. Identical X means little to no reaction.

X difference > 1.7 → Ionic bond 0.4 - 1.7 → Polar covalent < 0.4 → Nonpolar covalent

7-8Evaluate
Q2
Your friend claims to have seen a strong reaction between lithium and calcium. Determine whether this is plausible and justify using electronegativity.
Evaluate
▶
Answer
This is not plausible. Both lithium (X = 0.98) and calcium (X = 1.00) have very low electronegativity. The difference is approximately 0.02, far below the threshold for a strong reaction. Reactive collisions require one atom to strongly attract the electrons of the other. With nearly identical electronegativity, no significant electron transfer or sharing occurs and no strong reaction would be expected.
Reactivity & Special Groups (1, 2, 17, 18)
▶
  • Group 1 — Alkali metals: 1 valence electron. Very reactive — they want to lose that one electron. Reactivity increases going down the group (outer electron is further from nucleus, easier to remove).
  • Group 2 — Alkaline earth metals: 2 valence electrons. Reactive but less so than Group 1. Also form positive ions (2+ charge).
  • Group 17 — Halogens: 7 valence electrons, want to gain 1. Very reactive non-metals. Reactivity decreases going down the group (harder to attract an extra electron when the nucleus is more shielded).
  • Group 18 — Noble gases: Full outer shell (8 electrons). Extremely stable and essentially unreactive. Do not form compounds under normal conditions.
Key pattern: Metallic reactivity increases going DOWN a group. Non-metal reactivity increases going UP a group. This is linked to how easily electrons can be lost or gained.
Practice questions
5-6Explain
Q1
Explain why the reactivity of non-metals increases going higher up in a group, while metallic reactivity decreases going higher up in a group.
Explain
▶
Answer
Metals react by losing electrons. Going up a group, the atom gets smaller and the outer electrons are closer to the nucleus, held more tightly — they are harder to remove, so reactivity decreases. Non-metals react by gaining electrons. Going up a group, the atom is smaller and the nucleus is closer to where the new electron would go, attracting it more strongly — so reactivity increases.
Electron Shielding & Effective Nuclear Charge
▶

The nucleus pulls on all electrons with its positive charge. But inner shell electrons partially block this pull for the outer (valence) electrons. This blocking effect is called shielding.

The pull the valence electron actually feels is called the effective nuclear charge (Zeff). A simple estimate: Zeff = number of protons - number of inner (shielding) electrons.

Zeff = protons - inner electrons

Example: Sodium (Na) has 11 protons and 10 inner electrons. Zeff = 11 - 10 = +1. The valence electron feels a pull of only +1, even though the nucleus has +11 protons.

Going down a group: more shells = more shielding = lower Zeff on valence electrons = they are easier to remove or attract. This explains why electronegativity decreases down a group.

Practice questions
3-4Calculate
Q1
Calculate the effective nuclear charge on the valence electron of sodium (Na, atomic number 11).
Calculate
▶
Answer
Na has electron configuration 2, 8, 1. Inner electrons = 10. Zeff = 11 - 10 = +1
5-6Describe
Q2
Describe the change in properties of a fluorine atom if a proton were added to its nucleus.
Describe
▶
Answer
Adding a proton to fluorine (Z=9) would create neon (Z=10). Neon has a full outer shell of 8 electrons, making it a noble gas. It becomes non-reactive, has a smaller atomic radius (more protons pulling same electrons), much higher first ionization energy, and essentially zero electronegativity in practical terms since it does not form bonds.
Ions — Forming, Drawing & Naming
▶

An ion is an atom (or group of atoms) that has gained or lost electrons and therefore carries an electric charge. Metals lose electrons to form cations (positive ions). Non-metals gain electrons to form anions (negative ions).

  • Anions end in "-ide": F- = Fluoride, O2- = Oxide, S2- = Sulphide.
  • Cations keep the element name + "ion": Mg2+ = Magnesium ion.
  • Transition metals need a roman numeral to show charge: Fe3+ = Iron(III), Fe2+ = Iron(II).
Monovalent, bivalent, trivalent
  • Monovalent: forms 1+ or 1- ions (Na, K, F, Cl)
  • Bivalent: forms 2+ or 2- ions (Mg, Ca, O, S)
  • Trivalent: forms 3+ or 3- ions (Al, N, P)
Practice questions
3-4Define / Outline
Q1
Define the term "ion" and outline the difference between an anion and a cation, giving one named example of each.
Define / Outline
▶
Answer
An ion is an atom that has gained or lost electrons, giving it an electric charge. An anion is a negatively charged ion formed when an atom gains electrons (e.g. Cl-, chloride). A cation is a positively charged ion formed when an atom loses electrons (e.g. Na+, sodium ion).

▶

7-8Justify
Q3
Justify why FeCl3 must include a roman numeral in its name, but NaCl does not.
Justify
▶
Answer
Sodium (Group 1) can only ever form a Na+ ion — its charge is always +1 and is fully predictable from its position. Iron is a transition metal that can form Fe2+ or Fe3+ depending on the compound. Without the roman numeral specifying Fe3+, it would be impossible to determine the correct formula. NaCl needs no numeral because there is no ambiguity.
Taxonomy — KPCOFGS, Dichotomous Keys & Cladograms
▶

Scientists classify all life using a hierarchical system from broadest to most specific: Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species (KPCOFGS). All life is first divided into three Domains: Bacteria, Archaea, Eukaryota.

The scientific name of a species is written in italics using the Genus and Species: Panthera leo (lion), Homo sapiens (human).

Dichotomous key

A dichotomous key classifies an unknown organism by asking a series of yes/no questions about observable features. At each step, you choose between two options until you reach a classification. Each branch point splits the organisms into exactly two groups.

Cladograms (evolutionary trees)

A cladogram shows evolutionary relationships based on shared characteristics. Branch points represent common ancestors. The closer two species branch together, the more recently they shared a common ancestor.

Practice questions
3-4Describe
Q1

Describe
▶
Answer
A dichotomous key is a tool used to identify an unknown organism by working through a series of paired statements or yes/no questions about observable physical features. At each step, one of two options is chosen, leading progressively to a correct identification.

Unit 4 Chemical Bonding, Properties & Forces
Ionic Bonding — Cations, Anions & Dot-and-Cross Diagrams
▶

Ionic bonds form when a metal (electron donor) transfers electrons to a non-metal (electron acceptor). The metal becomes a positively charged cation; the non-metal becomes a negatively charged anion. The opposite charges attract, holding the ions together in a lattice structure.

How to draw a dot-and-cross diagram for an ionic compound
  1. Determine the electron configuration of the donor (metal) and acceptor (non-metal).
  2. Draw the electron shell diagrams side by side. Use dots for one atom and crosses for the other so you can track which electrons came from where.
  3. Draw a curly arrow from the donor to the acceptor showing the electron being transferred.
  4. Draw the new electron configurations of both ions after transfer.
  5. Place each ion in square brackets with its charge shown in the top right corner: [Na]+ [Cl]-
Key point: In ionic bonding, electrons are transferred (stolen), not shared. The curly arrow shows transfer, not sharing. Sharing belongs to covalent bonds.
Naming ionic compounds
  • Group 1 or 2 metal + non-metal: Metal name + non-metal with -ide. NaCl = Sodium chloride. MgO = Magnesium oxide.
  • Transition metal + non-metal: Metal name + charge in roman numerals + non-metal with -ide. FeCl3 = Iron(III) chloride.
  • Polyatomic ions: Treat as a single unit. CO32- = Carbonate. SO42- = Sulfate. NO3- = Nitrate.

Covalent Bonding — Sharing, Drawing & How Many Bonds
▶

Covalent bonds form between two non-metal atoms that share pairs of electrons. Each atom contributes one electron to a shared pair. Atoms share until they all have a full outer shell (usually 8 electrons — the octet rule; hydrogen is satisfied with 2).

How many bonds can each atom make?
  • Group 14 (C): 4 bonds
  • Group 15 (N, P): 3 bonds
  • Group 16 (O, S): 2 bonds
  • Group 17 (F, Cl, Br): 1 bond
  • Hydrogen (H): 1 bond (always on the outside)
Types of covalent bonds
  • Single bond: 1 shared pair of electrons (shown as one line: -)
  • Double bond: 2 shared pairs (shown as two lines: =)
  • Triple bond: 3 shared pairs (shown as three lines: ≡)
Structural formulae

Instead of drawing full dot-and-cross diagrams, covalent bonds can be shown as lines between atoms. CH4 (methane): H-C-H with two more H's above and below the C. Each line = one shared pair of electrons.

Polar vs nonpolar covalent bonds

If the two atoms sharing electrons have different electronegativities (difference 0.4-1.7), the electrons are not shared equally — the more electronegative atom pulls them closer, making the bond polar. Water is polar because oxygen (3.44) pulls much harder than hydrogen (2.20).

Metallic Bonding — Electron Sea, Pure Metals & Alloys
▶

In metallic bonding, metal atoms release their valence electrons into a shared "sea" of delocalized electrons. What remains are positive metal cations arranged in a regular lattice, surrounded by and attracted to the freely moving electrons. This is not sharing (covalent) and not transferring (ionic) — the electrons belong to no specific atom.

How to draw a metallic bond diagram
  • Draw a grid of positive cations (circles with + signs) in a regular repeating pattern.
  • Scatter small negative signs (e-) freely between and around all the cations.
  • Label the cations and label the free electrons as "delocalized electrons."
Pure metals vs alloys
  • Pure metals: All atoms the same size — layers can slide past each other easily. Malleable and ductile. Examples: gold (Au), copper (Cu), silver (Ag).
  • Alloys: Two or more different metals (or metal + non-metal) mixed. The different-sized atoms disrupt the regular lattice and prevent layers sliding — making the alloy harder and stronger. Examples: steel (Fe + C), bronze (Cu + Sn), brass (Cu + Zn), duralumin (Al + Cu).
Practice questions
1-2State
Q1
State what is meant by "delocalized electrons" in a metallic bond.
State
▶
Answer
Delocalized electrons are valence electrons that have been released from individual metal atoms and are free to move throughout the entire metallic structure, not belonging to any specific atom.
5-6Explain
Q2
Explain why alloys are generally harder than pure metals, using ideas about lattice structure.
Explain
▶
Answer
In a pure metal, all atoms are the same size and arranged in a regular lattice. Layers can slide past each other easily when force is applied, making the metal malleable. In an alloy, different-sized atoms are introduced into the lattice. These disrupt the regular pattern and prevent layers from sliding smoothly, making the material harder and stronger.

Practice questions
5-6Explain
Q1
Explain why ionic compounds are brittle when struck, using ideas about their lattice structure at the particle level.
Explain
▶
Answer
In an ionic lattice, positive and negative ions alternate in a regular pattern. When a force is applied, layers shift. This brings ions of the same charge next to each other. The repulsion between like charges is strong enough to split the crystal apart, causing the material to shatter rather than deform.
7-8Evaluate
Q2
Evaluate to what extent ionic compounds are more suitable as building materials than covalent compounds.
Evaluate
▶
Answer
Ionic compounds have high melting points and are hard, which are useful properties for construction. However, they are brittle and shatter under sudden stress rather than bending, making them poor for load-bearing applications. They can also dissolve in water over time. Metals (metallic bonding) are generally superior for structural use because they are hard, malleable, and ductile. Many covalent compounds (e.g. polymers, timber) are also widely used in construction. So ionic compounds have limited use as structural materials in practice.
Balancing Chemical Equations
▶

A chemical equation must have the same number of each type of atom on both sides. You balance by adding coefficients (numbers in front of formulas) — never by changing the subscripts inside a formula.

Step-by-step method
  1. Count the number of each atom on each side of the equation.
  2. Start with the element that appears in the fewest formulas (usually not H or O).
  3. Add a coefficient to balance that element.
  4. Recount all atoms after each change.
  5. Balance H and O last.
  6. Check that all atoms balance and all coefficients are the smallest whole numbers possible.
Diatomic elements: These elements exist as pairs of atoms when alone: H2, N2, F2, O2, I2, Cl2, Br2. Remember: "Have No Fear Of Ice Cold Beer."

Example: C3H8 + O2 → CO2 + H2O. Balance C first (put 3 in front of CO2), then H (put 4 in front of H2O), then count O on the right (3x2 + 4x1 = 10 O atoms), so put 5 in front of O2. Balanced: C3H8 + 5O2 → 3CO2 + 4H2O.

Forces — Balanced, Unbalanced & Adding Vectors
▶

A force is a push or pull that has both a size (magnitude, in Newtons) and a direction. Forces are vectors.

Balanced vs unbalanced forces
  • Balanced forces: Net force = 0 N. The object stays still OR moves at constant speed. Forces are equal in size and opposite in direction.
  • Unbalanced forces: Net force is not zero. The object accelerates (speeds up), decelerates (slows down), or changes direction.
F_net = sum of all forces (direction matters)
Adding and subtracting forces
  • Forces in the same direction: add them. 30 N right + 20 N right = 50 N right.
  • Forces in opposite directions: subtract. 30 N right + 20 N left = 10 N right (in the direction of the larger force).
Vectors in a coordinate system

A force vector can be written as (x, y) where x is the horizontal component and y is the vertical component. To add vectors, add x values together and y values together. To find the magnitude: |V| = sqrt(x2 + y2).

Normal force

Every surface pushes back against an object resting on it with a normal force (N), always perpendicular to the surface. A book on a flat desk: weight (mg) acts downward, normal force (N) acts upward. If the book is not moving, N = mg and forces are balanced.

Unit 5 Energy, Heat, Enthalpy & Organic Chemistry
Energy Recap, Heat Energy & Heating Curves
▶

Energy is the ability to do work — to cause change. The Law of Conservation of Energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed. Energy comes in many forms: kinetic, potential, chemical, thermal, electrical.

Temperature vs Heat
  • Temperature (T): Average kinetic energy of particles. Measured in Kelvin (K) or degrees Celsius (°C). An absolute property of the substance.
  • Heat (Q): Total thermal energy transferred between two objects at different temperatures. Measured in Joules (J). A relative quantity — it describes a transfer, not a state.
Heating curves

A heating curve plots temperature against heat energy added. When a substance is melting or boiling, temperature stays constant even though heat is being added — this is latent heat. When the substance is a solid, liquid, or gas heating up, temperature rises — this is sensible heat.

  • Flat sections on a heating curve = phase change (latent heat). The energy breaks intermolecular bonds, not increase KE.
  • Sloped sections = temperature rising (sensible heat).
  • A steeper slope means the substance heats up faster (lower specific heat capacity).
Practice questions

Differentiate
▶
Answer
Temperature is the average kinetic energy of particles in a substance, measured in K or °C. Heat is the total energy transferred between objects at different temperatures, measured in Joules. Temperature is a property of a substance; heat is an energy transfer between substances.

Specific Heat Capacity & Latent Heat Calculations
▶

Specific heat capacity (c) tells you how much energy is needed to raise 1 kg of a substance by 1 K. Water has a very high c (4182 J/kg/K), meaning it takes a lot of energy to heat. This is why the ocean moderates coastal climates.

Q = m x c x delta T
  • Q = heat energy (J)
  • m = mass (kg)
  • c = specific heat capacity (J/kg/K)
  • delta T = change in temperature (K or °C, same size)

Latent heat (L) is the energy absorbed or released during a phase change with no temperature change. Each material has a specific L value for each transition (melting, boiling).

Q = m x L
Remember: Use Q = mcT for heating/cooling within a phase. Use Q = mL at a phase change (flat section of heating curve).

Endothermic vs Exothermic & Enthalpy (delta H)
▶

Enthalpy (H) is the total energy stored in the bonds of a molecule. We can only measure changes in enthalpy (delta H), not H itself. Substances with lower enthalpy are more stable.

Exothermic reactions (delta H is negative)
  • Energy is released to the surroundings as heat.
  • Products have lower enthalpy than reactants.
  • The energy released forming new bonds is greater than the energy needed to break old bonds.
  • Examples: combustion, respiration, many oxidation reactions.
Endothermic reactions (delta H is positive)
  • Energy is absorbed from the surroundings.
  • Products have higher enthalpy than reactants.
  • More energy is needed to break old bonds than is released forming new ones.
  • Examples: photosynthesis, thermal decomposition, dissolving some salts.
Both types require activation energy first — you always need to put in some energy to break the initial bonds before a reaction can proceed, whether the overall reaction is exo or endothermic.
Practice questions
5-6Compare
Q1
Compare and contrast endothermic and exothermic reactions in terms of enthalpy change and energy flow.
Compare
▶
Answer
Both types of reaction require activation energy to get started and involve breaking and forming chemical bonds. In an exothermic reaction, more energy is released forming new bonds than is needed to break old ones — the products have lower enthalpy than reactants and delta H is negative. In an endothermic reaction, more energy is absorbed breaking old bonds than is released forming new ones — products have higher enthalpy than reactants and delta H is positive.
Calculating Delta H from Bond Energies
▶

Every bond has a bond energy — the energy needed to break it (or released when it forms). You can calculate the overall enthalpy change of a reaction if you know the bond energies of all bonds broken and formed.

delta H = energy in (bonds broken) - energy out (bonds formed)
  • Breaking bonds always absorbs energy (endothermic step).
  • Forming bonds always releases energy (exothermic step).
  • If delta H is negative: more energy released than absorbed — exothermic overall.
  • If delta H is positive: more energy absorbed than released — endothermic overall.

Worked example: H2 + Br2 → 2HBr. Bond energies: H-H = 432 kJ/mol, Br-Br = 193 kJ/mol, H-Br = 363 kJ/mol. Energy in = 432 + 193 = 625 kJ. Energy out = 2 x 363 = 726 kJ. Delta H = 625 - 726 = -101 kJ/mol (exothermic).

Practice questions
5-6Calculate
Q1
Using the worked example method, calculate delta H for the reaction N2 + 3H2 → 2NH3. Bond energies: N-N triple bond = 945 kJ/mol, H-H = 436 kJ/mol, N-H = 391 kJ/mol.
Calculate
▶
Answer
Bonds broken: 1 x N≡N (945) + 3 x H-H (3 x 436 = 1308) = 2253 kJ. Bonds formed: 2 x NH3 = 2 x (3 x N-H) = 6 x 391 = 2346 kJ. Delta H = 2253 - 2346 = -93 kJ/mol (exothermic).
Collision Theory, Rate of Reaction & Catalysts
▶

For a chemical reaction to happen, particles must collide with enough energy to overcome the activation energy barrier. Not every collision leads to a reaction — only those where the energy at the moment of collision is at or above the activation energy. These are called effective collisions.

Factors that increase reaction rate
  • Temperature: Particles move faster, collide more often, and more collisions have enough energy. Most important factor.
  • Concentration (solutions): More particles in the same space means more frequent collisions.
  • Pressure (gases): Same effect as concentration — squeezing the gas increases collision frequency.
  • Surface area (solids): Crushing a solid exposes more surface for collisions — rate increases.
  • Catalyst: Provides an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy. More collisions now have enough energy, so rate increases.
Catalysts in detail

A catalyst lowers the activation energy but is not consumed in the reaction — it is regenerated and can be used again. Catalysts do not change the enthalpy of reactants or products (delta H stays the same), only the route to get there. Enzymes are biological catalysts with a lock-and-key mechanism for specific substrates.

Practice questions
5-6Explain
Q1
Explain how increasing temperature increases the rate of reaction, using ideas from collision theory.
Explain
▶
Answer
Increasing temperature gives particles more kinetic energy, so they move faster. This means: (1) more collisions per second because particles cover more ground, and (2) a greater proportion of those collisions have energy above the activation energy threshold. Both effects increase the frequency of effective collisions and therefore the rate of reaction.

The statement is mostly incorrect. The light-dependent reactions of photosynthesis require light to split water and produce ATP and NADPH. Sustained glucose production through photosynthesis is not possible in the dark.
Organic Compounds, Hydrocarbons & Combustion
▶

An organic compound is any molecule containing both carbon and hydrogen atoms. Organic compounds form the basis of all living things and fossil fuels. They have covalent bonds and can be very large and diverse. Elements present are mostly C, H, O, N, P, S, and sometimes halogens.

Hydrocarbons

Hydrocarbons contain only carbon and hydrogen. They come in three homologous series based on bond type:

SeriesBondsSaturated?ExampleGeneral formula
AlkaneSingle bonds onlySaturatedMethane (CH4), Propane (C3H8)CnH(2n+2)
AlkeneContains a double bondUnsaturatedEthene (C2H4), Propene (C3H6)CnH(2n)
AlkyneContains a triple bondUnsaturatedEthyne (C2H2)CnH(2n-2)
Combustion of alkanes

Combustion is the reaction of a hydrocarbon with oxygen to produce carbon dioxide and water. Combustion is always exothermic — it releases energy as heat and light. This is why fossil fuels are used as energy sources.

Complete combustion: alkane + O2 → CO2 + H2O

Example: CH4 + 2O2 → CO2 + 2H2O
